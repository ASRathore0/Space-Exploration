Formation of Planets

Planets are thought to form from a nebula that collapses into a rotating disk of gas and dust. A protostar forms at the center, while planetesimals (small solid bodies) grow through accretion—where dust particles collide and stick together. Over time, these bodies become protoplanets and eventually planets. Once a star ignites, remaining gas and dust are cleared out, leaving behind planets and smaller bodies. Some moons form like planets, while others, like Triton, may be captured by gravity. Early planetary surfaces melt due to impacts and radioactive decay, causing differentiation (denser materials sink to form a core). New observations of exoplanetary systems suggest that a star's metal content influences its likelihood of forming planets. Metal-rich stars are more likely to have planetary systems.

Planets in the Solar System

The Solar System consists of eight planets in order of increasing distance from the Sun: Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune. Jupiter is the largest planet, with a mass 318 times that of Earth, while Mercury is the smallest, with only 0.055 Earth masses. Based on composition, planets are classified into three types. Terrestrial planets—Mercury, Venus, Earth, and Mars—are rocky and dense. Gas giants, Jupiter and Saturn, are composed mostly of hydrogen and helium, while ice giants, Uranus and Neptune, contain water, methane, and ammonia. In addition to planets, the Solar System contains dwarf planets, which are rounded by gravity but have not cleared their orbits. These include Ceres, Pluto, Eris, Haumea, and Makemake, among others. Ceres, located in the asteroid belt, is the smallest, while Pluto and Eris are the largest. Most dwarf planets reside beyond Neptune in the Kuiper Belt and scattered disc. They are composed mainly of ice and rock, unlike the rocky terrestrial planets. There are at least 19 planetary-mass moons, meaning they are large enough to take a spherical shape. Notable examples include Earth’s Moon, Jupiter’s Io, Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto, Saturn’s Titan, Enceladus, Uranus’s Titania, Oberon, Neptune’s Triton, and Pluto’s Charon. Some moons, like Europa and Titan, are of great scientific interest due to their potential subsurface oceans, which may harbor conditions suitable for life.

Exoplanets

An exoplanet is a planet outside the Solar System, with over 7,026 confirmed exoplanets as of July 2024. They vary in size from gas giants larger than Jupiter to Earth-sized rocky planets and even smaller. The first confirmed exoplanets were discovered in 1992 orbiting a pulsar, while the first around a main-sequence star was found in 1995 (51 Pegasi b). The Kepler space telescope has identified many Neptune-sized and smaller planets, including some in the habitable zone, where conditions could allow liquid water. Some unique exoplanet types include super-Earths, mini-Neptunes, and hot Jupiters, which orbit extremely close to their stars. Others, like COCONUTS-2b, have orbits much larger than any planet in the Solar System. Studies suggest that one in five Sun-like stars may have an Earth-sized planet in the habitable zone, raising the possibility of extraterrestrial life.

Attributes of Planets

Although each planet has unique characteristics, they share common dynamic properties, especially in their orbits. In the Solar System, planets orbit the Sun in the same direction as its rotation, but some exoplanets, like WASP-17b, orbit in the opposite direction. A planet's orbital period or year depends on its distance from the star—the farther it is, the longer its orbit. No planet has a perfectly circular orbit; instead, they follow elliptical paths, with the closest point to the star called perihelion and the farthest called aphelion. The eccentricity of an orbit determines its shape, with low eccentricity meaning a nearly circular orbit, while high eccentricity results in an elongated orbit, as seen in some exoplanets and comets. The semi-major axis defines the size of an orbit, and the inclination measures how tilted it is relative to a reference plane. In the Solar System, most planets have low inclination, but Pluto, Eris, and some comets have highly tilted orbits. Some moons also exhibit unique orbital behavior, such as Triton, which orbits Neptune retrograde, meaning in the opposite direction of the planet’s rotation.

History and Etymology

The concept of planets has evolved significantly throughout the history of astronomy, from the divine lights observed by ancient civilizations to the scientific understanding we have today. Early astronomers viewed planets as wandering stars, with the five classical planets, visible to the naked eye, having a significant impact on mythology, religious cosmology, and ancient astronomy. The Babylonians were the first known civilization to have a functional theory of the planets, with surviving texts such as the Babylonian Venus tablet and the MUL.APIN, which documented the motions of the Sun, Moon, and planets. The Greeks, initially less focused on the planets, developed a heliocentric system by Aristarchus of Samos in the 3rd century BC, though the geocentric model remained dominant until the Scientific Revolution. Medieval astronomers in India and the Islamic world built on these ideas, with figures like Aryabhata and Ibn al-Haytham contributing significant advancements. The Scientific Revolution in the 16th century shifted the definition of a planet from one that moved across the sky relative to fixed stars to a body that orbited the Sun. This change led to Earth being added to the list of planets, and the Sun removed. The discovery of Uranus in 1781, along with other major astronomical advancements, further expanded our understanding of planets, with new categories of "satellite planets" and "minor planets" like asteroids emerging. In the 20th century, Pluto was initially classified as the ninth planet, but further observations led to its reclassification as a dwarf planet in 2006 by the International Astronomical Union (IAU). The IAU's definition of a planet, which requires an object to orbit the Sun, have sufficient mass for a nearly round shape, and clear its orbital neighborhood, resulted in the Solar System being considered to have eight planets. However, the definition remains debated, with some astronomers advocating for a geophysical approach based on intrinsic properties like shape and mass. The discovery of exoplanets and their unique characteristics has added further complexity to the definition of what constitutes a planet, particularly when considering objects with masses approaching that of brown dwarfs or those formed through gas-cloud collapse.

Mythology and Naming

The naming of planets differs between those in the Solar System and exoplanets. Exoplanets are typically named after their parent star with an added designation for their order of discovery, such as Proxima Centauri b. The planets in the Solar System (other than Earth) are named based on ancient Babylonian, Greek, and Roman traditions. The Babylonians first named the planets after gods, with the Greeks and Romans adopting these names and associating them with their gods. For example, Venus was named after the goddess of love, Mars after the god of war, and Jupiter after the chief god. Earth’s name, however, is not derived from Greco-Roman mythology, but from Old English, meaning "ground" or "dirt." Different cultures use varying naming systems; in India, the planets are named after deities like Surya for the Sun and Shani for Saturn, while Persian, Chinese, and Hebrew systems have their own planetary names based on gods, elements, or descriptive terms. Additionally, modern moons and asteroids are named with associations to their parent planets or mythological figures.
